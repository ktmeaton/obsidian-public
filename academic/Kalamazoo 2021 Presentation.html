Introduction

Notes about the introduction section.

What is Ancient DNA?

  • DNA retrieved from skeletal remains (+soil and artifacts!)
  • Genetic evidence offers a window into past disease experience.
  • Can be used to study infectious diseases, like medieval plague.

Illustration by Martin Rowson

How is Ancient DNA applied?

  1. WHEN did plague first appear in human populations1 ?
  2. WHERE has plague appeared2 ?
  3. HOW has plague moved across the landscape3 ?

Guellil et al. (2020)

What evidence is needed?

Robust comparative datasets

  1. Extensive sampling across time and place.
  2. Careful curation of DNA sequences and metadata.

Fortunately, recent advances in DNA sequencing technology have facilitated efforts to conduct this extensive sampling on a Global scale, and data repositories continue to grow at unprecedented rates.

What are some obstacles?

  • This technological progress has created an avalanche of data.

  • Challenging to collect, curate, and present this information.

  • 1600+ publicly available plague genomes.

  • Only 200-300 are routinely used in comparative frameworks.

Data Curation

Process

  1. Cross-reference the automated database with primary sources.

    • Correct discrepancies and gather additional context.

  2. Standardize metadata fields for analysis.

    • Collection Location: Peru Chota, San Juan de Licupis
    • Collection Date: 1999-04-10
    • Collection Host: Neopsylla setosa

  3. Examine the DNA sequence data for quality.

Collection Location

  1. Select a geographic resolution that is appropriate for the study.

    • Country, Province*, City, etc.
Country (N=85) Province (N=85)

  1. Select a nomenclature system and coordinate reference.

    • Google Maps, MapBox, OpenStreetMap*, etc.
    • Raw: Peru Chota, San Juan de Licupis, Los Porongos
    • Standardized: Peru, Cajamarca, Province of Chota, San Juan de Licupis
    • Coordinates: -6.25, -78.833333

Collection Date

  1. Select a temporal resolution that is appropriate for the study.

    • Millennia, Century, Year*, Month, Day, etc.
    • Raw: 1999-04-10
    • Standardized: 1999

  2. Research how your analytical tools will handle uncertainty.

    • Raw: Between -2876 BCE and -2677 BCE.
    • Standardized: -2776 BCE +/ 100

  3. Research how your analytical tools will handle years BCE.

    • Raw: -2776 BCE +/ 100
    • Standardized: 4797 YBP +/100

Collection Host

  1. Select a biological resolution that is appropriate for the study.

    • Family, Genus, Species etc.
    • Human vs. Non-Human*

  2. This is an extremely complex field because:

    • Plague Ecology: Wild Rodents – Insects – Other Mammals
    • Taxonomic identification of a wide range of mammals is hard!
    • “Meriones libycus” vs. “rat”

DNA Sequence Quality

  1. Select a genetic resolution that is appropriate for the study.

    • Millennia, Century, Decade*, Year, Month, Day, etc.

  2. Process the DNA sequences using standardized workflows.

  1. Verify the results are congruent with the original publications.
